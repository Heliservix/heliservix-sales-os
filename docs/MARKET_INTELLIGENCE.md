# Market Intelligence

## Purpose

Market intelligence turns public and internal signals into commercial action. For HeliServiX, useful intelligence is not generic news. It is information that helps identify fleet owners, vessel activity, procurement timing, regional opportunity, operational risk, or relationship openings for helicopter-supported tuna operations.

## Intelligence Types

- Company news: acquisitions, fleet expansion, leadership changes, financing, port investments.
- Vessel signals: new vessels, capacity changes, flag changes, ownership changes, port activity.
- Regulatory signals: fishing rules, licensing, closures, sanctions, observer requirements, safety mandates.
- Operational signals: seasonal patterns, port congestion, fishery activity, maintenance windows, logistics issues.
- Competitor signals: helicopter support providers, spotting alternatives, pricing pressure, contract awards.
- Relationship signals: executive appointments, public interviews, trade associations, conference attendance.
- Risk signals: accidents, legal disputes, environmental incidents, compliance issues.

## Source Governance

Every intelligence item must include:

- Source title.
- Source URL or file reference.
- Source type.
- Publisher or origin.
- Publication date when available.
- Access date.
- Language.
- Summary.
- Extracted entities.
- Confidence score.
- Analyst review status.

Source types:

- Public website.
- Trade publication.
- Government registry.
- Port authority notice.
- Industry association.
- Company website.
- Social media.
- Internal note.
- Customer communication.
- Document upload.

## Confidence Levels

Suggested scale:

- Verified: official source or directly confirmed by trusted contact.
- High: reputable source with clear entity match.
- Medium: plausible source but incomplete detail.
- Low: unconfirmed signal, indirect mention, or weak entity match.
- Rejected: reviewed and determined unreliable or irrelevant.

Low-confidence intelligence can inform research tasks but should not trigger external claims in emails, proposals, or reports.

## Entity Linking

Every intelligence item should be linked where relevant:

- Company.
- Fleet owner.
- Vessel.
- Contact.
- Country.
- Port.
- Opportunity.
- Contract.
- Helicopter or operational constraint.

An intelligence item can link to multiple entities. Each link should have relevance and confidence notes.

## Intelligence Workflow

1. Capture source.
2. Extract summary and entities.
3. Assign source type and confidence.
4. Link to relevant records.
5. Review or reject.
6. Create recommended action when commercially useful.
7. Track whether the action was accepted, dismissed, or completed.

## Recommended Actions

Examples:

- Create account for newly discovered fleet operator.
- Update vessel capacity estimate.
- Create opportunity for upcoming campaign.
- Prioritize outreach before season start.
- Prepare executive briefing.
- Add competitor risk note.
- Schedule follow-up with known contact.
- Request operational feasibility review.

## AI Use in Intelligence

AI may summarize sources, extract names, suggest entity matches, translate content, and propose actions. AI must not assign final verified status, invent missing facts, or send intelligence-derived claims externally without review.

## Dashboards

Market intelligence dashboards should show:

- New high-confidence signals.
- Unreviewed intelligence queue.
- Signals by country and port.
- Signals by company and vessel.
- Recommended actions.
- Competitor activity.
- Expiring or stale intelligence.

## Data Retention

Keep source summaries, metadata, and links. Do not store copyrighted source text beyond legally acceptable excerpts or internal summaries. For sensitive information, restrict access and document why the information is business-relevant.
